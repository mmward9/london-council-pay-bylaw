# PACT Bylaw v13 — Deep Research Legal Audit
### City of London, Ontario — Council Compensation By-law Amendment
**Audit Date:** May 23, 2026 | **Prepared for:** PACT Campaign | **Scope:** Comprehensive legal, drafting, and political audit of v13 operative text

***

## Executive Summary

**Overall Assessment: CONDITIONAL PASS — High Legal Confidence, Two Drafting Fixes Required**

PACT v13 is a materially stronger instrument than v12. Its core architecture — a persistent, multiplicative accumulated-decrement ledger — is well-suited to survive the structural gap v12 left open (base-setting resolutions wiping accumulated decreases). After exhaustive legal analysis:

- **Municipal Act authority:** CONFIRMED. The broad authority under ss. 5(3), 8(1), 8(2), 11(2)(1), and 11(2)(3) of the *Municipal Act, 2001*, read with ss. 283 and 312, supports this form of by-law. The mechanism is a procedural/formulaic expression of the power to set and govern remuneration — a well-established sphere.
- **MCIA s. 4(i) exemption:** CONFIRMED for all three relevant vote-types, with a nuanced note on the annual rating by-law.
- **Auer/Vavilov reasonableness:** CONFIRMED. v13 bears the hallmarks of rationality, transparency, and intelligibility; the presumption of validity applies; the by-law is consistent with the enabling statute's purposes.
- **Persistence across base-setting resolutions (the key v13 question):** CONFIRMED with analysis. The fetter-on-discretion doctrine is directed at administrative decision-makers exercising discretion in individual cases, not at Council legislatively structuring the formula by which it pays itself. Section 4.3(g)'s explicit "regardless of the value of any base compensation" language is legally sound.
- **Multiplicative-decrement structure:** CONFIRMED. No new exposure under the *Municipal Act*, *Public Sector Salary Disclosure Act* (PSSDA), or *Pay Equity Act* is introduced by the multiplicative vs. additive distinction.
- **Strong-mayor veto:** CONFIRMED. Same low-risk profile as v12. O. Reg. 580/22 prescribes housing/infrastructure priorities only; compensation policy is not prescribed.
- **ESA/minimum wage/pay-to-zero:** CONFIRMED. Elected municipal officials are not "employees" under the *Employment Standards Act, 2000*; the zero-floor is legally sustainable.
- **Drafting defects identified:** TWO ISSUES require fixing: (1) The "fiscal year" vs. "calendar year" alignment with the City's January 1–December 31 fiscal year should be made explicit. (2) The phrase "for that fiscal year" in s. 4.3(b) is ambiguous about *when* the update occurs relative to the January 1 pay reset under s. 4.2.
- **Political vulnerability:** Council opponents will frame v13 as a constitutional fetter on legislative discretion and a punitive mechanism designed to micromanage fiscal decisions. The PACT response must foreground the democratic fiscal-accountability rationale and the floor-at-zero safeguard.

## Section 1 — Municipal Act Authority

**Finding: CONFIRMED**

### Statutory Basis

The *Municipal Act, 2001* grants the City of London broad authority to govern its own affairs through by-law. The key provisions are:

- **Section 5(3)**: "A municipal power … shall be exercised by by-law unless the municipality is specifically authorized to do otherwise." This provision establishes the by-law as the correct instrument — v13 uses a by-law to modify the *Policy*, satisfying this requirement.[1]
- **Section 8(1)**: Directs that municipal powers "shall be interpreted broadly so as to confer broad authority on the municipality." This is the foundational interpretive instruction that makes a broad reading of compensation authority appropriate.[1]
- **Section 8(2)**: Confirms that doubt about the scope of a power shall be resolved in favour of the municipality.[1]
- **Section 11(2)(1)**: Grants authority over "governance structure of the municipality." Council compensation is core governance structure — it relates to the conditions and remuneration of the institution's elected members.[1]
- **Section 11(2)(3)**: Grants authority over "accountability and transparency of the municipality." A mechanism that links pay to fiscal behaviour is directly within the accountability sphere.[1]
- **Section 283**: The remuneration and expenses section of the Act explicitly authorises a municipality to pass by-laws governing remuneration of council members. Section 283 does not prescribe a mandatory floor, a mandatory formula, or any restriction on linking pay to fiscal performance.[1]
- **Section 312(2)**: Governs the annual rating by-law under which the municipal tax levy is set. Section 4.3(a)(i)–(ii) adopt and track this instrument as the operative input for the levy change percentage — making the mechanism anchored to an already-defined, published, statutory instrument.[1]

### Why a Parallel-Decrement Ledger Is Authorised

The accumulated decrement is not a separate tax, charge, or penalty on third parties. It is an internal formula element that modulates the quantum of remuneration payable to elected officials. The Act does not require remuneration to be set as a simple dollar figure or a single formula. It has never been interpreted to prohibit multi-component calculations. Ontario municipalities routinely use index-linked, multi-variable compensation formulas (including the existing s. 4.2 mechanism in London's own Policy). Adding a multiplicative modifier is a difference in degree, not in kind.[2]

Under the *Auer*/*Vavilov* reasonableness framework (see Section 3), a court reviewing v13 asks whether the by-law falls within the scope of the municipality's authority under the enabling legislation, interpreted broadly and purposively. Given the explicit broad-authority instructions in s. 8, the answer is clearly yes.[3]

### Annual Rating By-law Tie-In (s. 312(2))

The definition of "annual rating by-law" in s. 4.3(a)(i) directly mirrors the language of s. 312(2), which authorises the passing of a by-law "that sets the rates of taxation upon the assessment of the lands and premises in the City of London for the purpose of raising the general local municipality levy." This statutory grounding is careful and defensible. No gap exists between the legislative authority for the input variable and the by-law's invocation of it.[1]

## Section 2 — MCIA Section 4(i) Self-Remuneration Exemption

**Finding: CONFIRMED (all three vote-types), with important procedural note**

### The Exemption

Section 4(i) of the *Municipal Conflict of Interest Act* (MCIA) provides that ss. 5, 5.2, and 5.3 (the disclosure and non-participation obligations) do not apply to a pecuniary interest "in respect of an allowance for attendance at meetings, or any other allowance, honorarium, remuneration, salary or benefit to which the member may be entitled by reason of being a member." This is a blanket exemption: all 15 members of London City Council have a direct pecuniary interest in the v13 by-law, and all 15 are exempted.[4]

### (a) Vote on the v13 By-law Itself

All 15 members may vote freely on the v13 by-law under s. 4(i). The by-law governs their "remuneration … by reason of being a member." This is precisely what the exemption was designed to cover — the Legislature recognised that prohibiting councillors from voting on their own compensation would make self-governance impossible.[5][4]

### (b) Future Annual Rating By-law Votes

This is the v13-specific question that did not arise under v12. Each year, when Council votes on the annual rating by-law under s. 312(2), the adopted levy percentage will mechanically feed into the accumulated-decrement calculation and affect councillors' pay.

The critical legal question is: does the pecuniary interest in the annual rating by-law (as an input into the pay formula) constitute a pecuniary interest "in respect of … remuneration … to which the member may be entitled by reason of being a member"?

The answer is **yes, the s. 4(i) exemption applies**, but the reasoning requires care:

1. The annual rating by-law vote is primarily about tax policy for ratepayers — not about councillor pay. Councillors' interest in their own remuneration is a *secondary*, *derivative* effect of the levy vote, mediated through the s. 4.3 formula.
2. Section 4(j) of the MCIA provides a separate catch-all: the exemption applies where the interest is "in common with electors generally." All councillors, as property owners and taxpayers in London, have a property-tax interest that is shared with the general elector population. This further removes any MCIA concern.[4]
3. The s. 4(k) "remote or insignificant" exemption would also likely apply — a councillor's incremental personal interest in slightly affecting their own accumulated decrement via a levy vote is speculative and attenuated.

**Practical recommendation:** While the legal analysis supports full participation, prudence dictates that staff or legal counsel prepare a brief memo documenting the applicable exemptions for reference in the event of any post-vote challenge. This is a belt-and-suspenders measure, not a legal necessity.

### (c) Future Votes to Amend or Repeal Section 4.3

Any future vote to amend or repeal s. 4.3 is unambiguously within s. 4(i). It concerns remuneration directly. All 15 members may participate without disclosure.[4]

## Section 3 — Auer v. Auer (2024 SCC 36) Reasonableness Standard

**Finding: CONFIRMED**

### The Auer Framework

In *Auer v. Auer*, 2024 SCC 36, and its companion *TransAlta Generation Partnership v. Alberta*, 2024 SCC 37, the Supreme Court of Canada confirmed that the *Vavilov* reasonableness standard presumptively applies to judicial review challenges to the *vires* of subordinate legislation, including municipal by-laws. Subordinate legislation benefits from a presumption of validity. The challenger bears the burden of establishing unreasonableness.[6][7][3]

A reviewing court asks: (1) Does the by-law bear the hallmarks of reasonableness — justification, transparency, and intelligibility? (2) Is it untenable in light of the factual and legal constraints bearing on it? The reviewing court may not set aside subordinate legislation merely because it is "unnecessary, unwise or ineffective in practice."[3]

### v13's Performance Against the Auer Framework

**Justification:** The by-law's stated logic — linking councillor pay to the fiscal decisions that councillors make about the property tax levy — is rationally connected to the accountability and transparency goals that s. 11(2)(3) of the *Municipal Act* expressly authorises. The causal chain is direct and intelligible: if Council raises the levy, councillors earn proportionally less; if Council holds or cuts the levy, the decrement erodes toward zero.[1]

**Transparency:** Section 4.3(e) requires the City Treasurer to (i) calculate the levy change percentage and accumulated decrement annually, (ii) maintain a record with the City Clerk, and (iii) publish the current year's values and resulting pay figures in the annual budget documents. This is an exceptionally high transparency standard, superior to most compensation by-laws currently in force in Ontario.

**Intelligibility:** The formula in s. 4.3(b) is a simple max(0, prior_decrement + levy_change_pct) operation. It is deterministic, parameter-free (no discretion required in application), and publicly reproducible from the published budget. This is more intelligible than many property tax calculation mechanisms already in force.

**Consistency with Enabling Statute Purposes:** The *Municipal Act*, s. 2, describes municipalities as "responsible and accountable governments." A mechanism that ties councillor compensation to fiscal responsibility is well within the purposive scope of a municipality acting to govern itself accountably. Under *Auer/Vavilov*, courts are not to second-guess the policy wisdom of this linkage — only its legal scope.[3][1]

**Conclusion:** v13 is not an "egregious case" that would warrant judicial intervention. It is clearly intra vires and passes the reasonableness standard with significant margin.[7]

## Section 4 — Persistence Across Base-Setting Resolutions

**Finding: CONFIRMED — with analysis of the fetter doctrine**

This is v13's most novel and legally significant feature, and the question that v12 could not answer. The answer is that v13's persistence mechanism is lawful.

### The Fetter-on-Legislative-Discretion Doctrine

The doctrine provides that an administrative body exercising a discretionary power must exercise that discretion in each individual case and cannot commit in advance to a fixed outcome that disables case-by-case judgment. The paradigm cases are immigration officers bound by policy guidelines rather than law, or zoning boards that pre-commit to particular land use outcomes. The principle is that discretion must remain genuine — the decision-maker must "turn their mind to the specific circumstances."[8]

### Why v13 Is Not a Fetter

The fetter doctrine is directed at **administrative discretion in individual cases**, not at **legislative self-structuring**. There is a fundamental distinction between:

1. A Council resolution that attempts to commit a future Council to always vote a specific way on a specific future question (which would be an unlawful fetter, if possible at all), and
2. A Council by-law that establishes a *formula* by which pay will be calculated, while leaving Council entirely free to (a) set the levy at any amount it chooses, (b) set any base compensation it chooses, and (c) amend or repeal the formula by-law through the normal legislative process.

v13 is unambiguously of the second type. Section 4.3(g) is explicit: the by-law "does not restrict or alter Council's authority to set the property tax levy in any year, and does not restrict or alter Council's authority to set or update the base compensation." Council retains full legislative discretion over both inputs. The accumulated decrement is not a constraint on what Council *decides* — it is a consequence (in remuneration) of what Council *does decide*.

The closer analogy is to a municipality's own financial policies that have prospective effect on future budgets — e.g., a reserve fund policy that requires future councils to maintain a minimum reserve level. Ontario courts have consistently upheld such self-structuring policy mechanisms as lawful exercises of the policy-making sphere, provided the restraint is the result of a properly-passed by-law and future councils retain the ability to repeal or amend by the same process.[1]

The Young & Anderson analysis of legislative powers and fettering confirms that the doctrine requires specific statutory authority to overcome the baseline prohibition on binding future councils in their *legislative exercise of judgment*. v13 does not bind Council's legislative judgment — it structures the remuneration outcome as a function of Council's *freely-made* levy decisions. This is constitutionally and legally materially different.[9]

### Ontario Jurisprudence

No Ontario court has directly addressed a persistent-state-variable compensation mechanism of this design. The closest analogues are:
- The validity of index-linked compensation formulas in Ontario municipalities (broadly upheld as proper exercises of s. 283 authority).
- The Young & Anderson paper on repeal, rescission, and reconsideration, which confirms that any Council may repeal or amend a by-law through the normal legislative process, and that prior by-laws do not bind future councils except through properly enacted successor legislation.[10]

The absence of contrary jurisprudence — combined with the broad authority provisions of ss. 8(1) and 8(2) — strongly supports the mechanism's validity.

## Section 5 — Multiplicative-Decrement Structure Legality

**Finding: CONFIRMED — no new exposure vs. v12**

### Municipal Act

The *Municipal Act* contains no requirement that compensation modifiers be additive rather than multiplicative. The Act grants broad authority to govern remuneration under s. 283, and ss. 8(1) and 8(2) direct broad interpretation of all powers. A multiplicative modifier is simply a different mathematical function applied to the same input set. No statutory provision is violated.[1]

### Public Sector Salary Disclosure Act (PSSDA)

The PSSDA requires disclosure of salary paid to employees earning $100,000 or more. The Act defines "employee" to include "a holder of office elected or appointed under the authority of an Act of Ontario." The City of London is a "public sector" employer under the PSSDA.[11]

Importantly, the PSSDA requires disclosure of what is actually *paid*, not of any notional pre-decrement figure. v13 changes the *amount* paid, not the disclosure obligation. If the final remuneration after applying the accumulated decrement exceeds $100,000, the actual paid amount must be disclosed. If the actual amount falls below $100,000 (which could occur with a large accumulated decrement), no PSSDA disclosure obligation arises for that year's remuneration — but this is a consequence of lower actual pay, not a PSSDA violation.[11]

The PSSDA creates no right of an employee to a minimum salary at any level, and imposes no restriction on the design of compensation formulas. No PSSDA exposure arises from the multiplicative structure.[11]

### Pay Equity Act

The *Pay Equity Act* applies to employers and employees in Ontario to achieve and maintain pay equity based on gender. Municipal councillors are not "employees" within the meaning of the Pay Equity Act; their compensation is set by by-law under s. 283 of the *Municipal Act*. The Pay Equity Office has consistently treated elected officials as outside the scope of the Act. No Pay Equity Act exposure arises.[12][13]

### Employment Standards Act (ESA)

The ESA, s. 3 and the regulations thereunder, expressly exempt "holders of political, religious, or judicial office and holders of elected office in organizations" from the Act's minimum-wage and other employment standards provisions. Municipal councillors are holders of elected office and are not "employees" under the ESA. The ESA's minimum wage floor does not apply to them. No ESA exposure.[14]

### Conclusion

The multiplicative structure introduces no new legal exposure relative to v12's additive structure. The choice between the two is purely mathematical and design-driven.

## Section 6 — Strong-Mayor Veto Exposure

**Finding: CONFIRMED — same low-risk profile as v12**

### The Veto Mechanism

Section 284.11 of the *Municipal Act, 2001* grants the Mayor of a designated municipality (London is designated — Item 106 on Schedule 1 of O. Reg. 530/22) the power to veto a by-law if the Mayor is of the opinion that the by-law "could potentially interfere with a provincial priority."[15][16][1]

The prescribed provincial priorities are set out in O. Reg. 580/22, and are limited to:[17]
1. Building 1.5 million new residential units by December 31, 2031.
2. Constructing and maintaining infrastructure to support housing, including transit, roads, utilities, and servicing.

Council compensation policy has no connection to either prescribed priority. The Mayor's veto power under s. 284.11 is conditioned on an opinion that the by-law "could potentially interfere" with these housing/infrastructure priorities. No reasonable basis exists for such an opinion with respect to a councillor pay formula.

Additionally, O. Reg. 530/22 s. 5(1) expressly provides that s. 284.11 does not apply with respect to by-laws under ss. 289 and 290 (the budget by-laws). The compensation policy by-law is a separate instrument from the annual budget, and this exclusion does not directly apply to it — but it reinforces that the veto power is budget-adjacent in its focus, not compensation-adjacent.[16]

The 2/3 override threshold (10 of 15 members) is unchanged from v12. Given the 9-6 and 10-5 votes on the 2025-C06 resolution, a veto override is achievable (barely, on the weaker clause) but not assured, making a post-veto override the risk scenario — albeit a remote one.[18][16]

### Note on the Annual Rating By-law

The Mayor has budget-veto authority over council resolutions amending the proposed budget (O. Reg. 530/22 s. 7(6)). The annual rating by-law (s. 312(2)) is the instrument that operationalises the adopted budget for tax purposes. While the rating by-law itself is not a budget resolution and not directly covered by the s. 284.11 veto, any Council resolution amending the Mayor's proposed budget could be vetoed if the Mayor believes it interferes with housing priorities. This is a budget-process risk unrelated to v13 specifically — it is inherent to the strong-mayor framework.[16]

## Section 7 — Section 4.2 Independence

**Finding: CONFIRMED — language is unambiguous, with a recommended clarifying fix**

### The Language

Section 4.3(c) states: "For greater certainty, the operation of section 4.2 in any fiscal year is unaffected by the accumulated decrement under this section." Section 4.3(c)(i) instructs the Treasurer to compute the pre-decrement remuneration "without reference to the accumulated decrement." These are among the clearest independence-preserving expressions available in drafting.

### Court/Tribunal Interpretation Risk

A future interpreter could theoretically argue that even though s. 4.2 nominally operates independently, the practical effect of the decrement means that the "base" for s. 4.2 calculations must be interpreted as the post-decrement amount (to avoid compounding). This argument would fail because:
1. The operative text is explicit: s. 4.2 calculations use the pre-decrement base.
2. The example in the worked section demonstrates clean separation — s. 4.2 produces one figure, then the decrement is applied on top.
3. The s. 4.3(h) internal priority clause makes s. 4.3 prevail over any conflict with other s. 4 provisions, including s. 4.2 — but only as to interpretation conflicts relating to "annual adjustment, base compensation, and levy-linkage."

**Recommended drafting fix:** Add to s. 4.3(c)(i) the phrase "and which does not include any reduction attributable to the accumulated decrement from prior or current fiscal years" after the first reference to section 4.2. This forecloses any compounding-base argument with mathematical precision.

## Section 8 — Treasurer's Calculation Duty Under 4.3(e)

**Finding: CONFIRMED — duty is binding, mandamus is available but requires demand-and-refusal**

### Nature of the Obligation

Section 4.3(e) imposes three duties on the City Treasurer: (i) calculate the levy change percentage and accumulated decrement annually; (ii) maintain a record with the City Clerk; and (iii) include the values in the published annual budget documents. These are expressed as mandatory obligations ("shall"), not as discretionary powers.

Under Ontario administrative law, a municipal officer who fails to perform a mandatory statutory or by-law obligation may be compelled by an application for mandamus to the Superior Court of Justice. The test for mandamus includes: (1) a public legal duty to act; (2) the duty is owed to the applicant; (3) there has been a demand for performance and a refusal; and (4) no adequate alternative remedy exists.[19][20]

### Who Can Enforce

- **Councillors**: Any councillor has a direct legal interest in having their remuneration correctly calculated and documented. Assuming demand-and-refusal, mandamus would be available.
- **Members of the public**: The duty to publish in the annual budget documents (s. 4.3(e)(iii)) runs in favour of the public as well as councillors. A ratepayer with standing in the City's budget process would have a colourable claim, subject to the demand-and-refusal requirement.[21][20]

### Consequence of Non-Compliance

Failure to calculate and document the accumulated decrement does not affect the legal operation of the by-law — the decrement still accrues as a matter of law. However, it creates an administrative evidentiary problem: without the Treasurer's record, there is no authoritative published figure against which to reconcile payroll. In practice, non-compliance is most likely to occur through inadvertence (e.g., failure to update the record in an election year or during turnover in the Treasurer's office), not through intentional refusal.

**Recommended addition:** A clause specifying that in the event of a failure to calculate or document the decrement on time, the calculation shall be made as soon as practicable and shall be retroactively applied to remuneration for the relevant fiscal year, with any over- or under-payment reconciled in the next payment period. This prevents the calculation duty from becoming a de facto nullification mechanism.

## Section 9 — No-Cap-on-Decrement Exposure

**Finding: CONFIRMED — the unbounded decrement is legally sustainable, with important analysis**

### ESA Minimum Wage: Not Applicable

As noted in Section 5, municipal elected officials are not "employees" under the ESA and are exempt from minimum wage requirements. There is no statutory floor on councillor remuneration below which the decrement cannot operate. The decrement may lawfully drive councillor pay to any level, including zero.[14]

### Charter Section 15 Equality Challenge

A hypothetical challenge under s. 15 of the *Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms* (equality rights) would require showing that the accumulated decrement: (1) creates a distinction based on an enumerated or analogous ground; and (2) imposes burdens in a way that reinforces, perpetuates, or exacerbates disadvantage.[22]

Neither element is established here. The decrement applies uniformly to all 15 elected officials — it does not distinguish on the basis of race, sex, age, disability, or any analogous ground. It applies to all councillors equally, regardless of personal characteristics. A section 15 challenge would have no prospect of success.[23]

### Section 7 Charter: Life, Liberty, Security

A s. 7 challenge (right to life, liberty, and security of the person) could theoretically be brought if the pay-to-zero scenario threatened a councillor's ability to continue serving (deprivation of right to hold public office). However, s. 7 protections do not extend to economic interests absent a significant nexus to life, liberty, or security, and the Supreme Court has consistently declined to extend s. 7 to property or economic rights. A theoretical pay-to-zero scenario would require sustained double-digit levy increases for many consecutive years — a highly unlikely political scenario that would itself trigger political correction long before the decrement reached 100%.[23]

### Ultra Vires Concern: Pay to Zero

The *Municipal Act* does not require that councillors be paid any minimum amount. Section 283 authorises remuneration and expenses but does not mandate them. A municipality could theoretically set remuneration at zero by explicit resolution. If it can do so directly, it can do so through a formula that produces zero. The pay-to-zero outcome is therefore intra vires, even if extreme.

**Design note:** The intentional absence of an upper cap on the decrement is a political design choice. While legally sustainable, it creates a legitimate reputational risk (see Section 17). A practical safety valve in the form of a maximum decrement of, say, 50% or 75% would reduce this exposure without materially diminishing the mechanism's fiscal accountability function. This is flagged as a design option, not a legal requirement.

## Section 10 — Pay-Floor-at-Zero Treatment

**Finding: CONFIRMED — the zero-floor language is sufficient**

Section 4.3(c) states: "Where the accumulated decrement equals or exceeds 100, the final remuneration payable for that fiscal year is zero."

This language is clear and unambiguous. It prevents the formula from producing a negative remuneration figure (which would be incoherent and potentially constitute an unlawful levy on councillors). The zero-floor is consistent with the design intent: PACT constrains pay downward but does not require councillors to pay the city. Combined with the s. 4.3(b) floor-at-zero on the accumulated decrement itself, the mechanism is internally consistent.

**One minor gap:** The by-law does not address whether a councillor whose annual remuneration is zero for a given year continues to receive non-cash benefits (benefits, OMERS, expense account, technology). These items are part of the overall compensation package but are typically governed by separate policies. As drafted, v13 defines "remuneration" in s. 4.3(a)(v) to mean "base compensation … and any additional stipend or allowance paid … under this Policy." Non-cash benefits governed by separate insurance policies and the OMERS framework fall outside "this Policy" and thus are not affected by the accumulated decrement. This is likely the correct design intent — benefit entitlement as an elected official is a separate matter from cash remuneration. The by-law is silent on this point, which is appropriate.

## Section 11 — Council Escape Routes

**Finding: CONFIRMED — v13 closes the escape routes identified in v12, subject to analysis**

### (a) Repealing Section 4.3 Entirely

Any properly-constituted Council may repeal s. 4.3 by passing an amendment to the Policy by-law under the same process used to enact it — a majority vote at a properly-noticed meeting. A repeal by-law would require:[10][1]
- Notice on the agenda (typically 24 hours to two weeks, per the City's Procedure By-law A-61).
- A majority vote of the 15-member Council.
- No special quorum requirement beyond normal (8 of 15 for quorum).

**Strong-mayor veto risk on repeal:** The Mayor could veto a repeal by-law if he/she opines it interferes with a provincial priority. For the reasons in Section 6, no such opinion is tenable for a compensation policy amendment. The veto risk on repeal is as low as on the original enactment.

**Override:** If vetoed, Council could override with 10 of 15 votes (2/3). Given the 10-5 margin on the 2025-C06 resolution's weaker clause (Part (d)), this is achievable but not assured in a future political composition.[18][16]

### (b) Passing a Base-Setting Resolution That Purports to Bypass the Decrement

Section 4.3(d) states that the accumulated decrement "persists across, and continues to be applied after, any resolution, by-law, or amendment that establishes, resets, updates, or otherwise modifies the base compensation." Section 4.3(g) states that the decrement "applies regardless of the value of any base compensation set by Council." Section 4.3(h) establishes internal priority of s. 4.3 over all other s. 4 provisions relating to annual adjustment and base compensation.

These three provisions work together to close the gap. A resolution that merely resets the base (as 2025-C06 Part (d) does) cannot bypass the decrement because ss. 4.3(d) and (g) expressly apply the decrement to whatever new base results. The only mechanism by which future Council could escape the decrement is an express amendment or repeal of s. 4.3 itself (which requires a proper bylaw process, as noted in s. 4.3(g) in-fine and s. 4.3(f)).

**Conclusion:** The bypass attempt via base-setting resolution is foreclosed. This is v13's central architectural improvement over v12.

### (c) Mid-Term Amendment Attempts (Non-Repeal)

A future Council could attempt to amend s. 4.3 to: (i) reset the accumulated decrement to zero; (ii) cap the decrement at a lower maximum; (iii) change the formula; or (iv) exclude certain levy-increase years from the calculation.

Any such amendment requires a proper by-law with notice and a majority vote — the same process as full repeal. There is no heightened procedural protection against amendment: v13 is not entrenched against amendment in any way that the *Municipal Act* would enforce beyond the standard by-law process. A simple majority of Council (8 of 15) can amend at any time.[1]

**Key distinction from repeal:** An amendment that resets the accumulated decrement to zero mid-term is functionally a partial repeal of the mechanism's accumulated effect. It is procedurally valid (any Council can do this), but it would be politically exposed as an obvious self-serving use of legislative power — precisely the kind of visible accountability violation that PACT is designed to make salient.

## Section 12 — 2028 Re-Peg Interaction

**Finding: CONFIRMED — v13 fully survives the 2028 re-peg**

Resolution 2025-C06 Part (d) directs the 2028 re-peg of base compensation to the 70th percentile of 2025 income from the 2026 Census data. This is a base-resetting event — the same type of event that rendered v12 vulnerable.[24]

Under v13:
1. The 2028 re-peg fires, setting a new base (e.g., $118,500 in the worked example).
2. Section 4.3(c)(i) computes the pre-decrement remuneration for the 2028 fiscal year using the new base of $118,500 (plus any s. 4.2 annual adjustments applicable for 2028).
3. Section 4.3(c)(ii) multiplies the result by (1 − accumulated_decrement ÷ 100).
4. Sections 4.3(d) and (g) make explicit that the decrement survives and applies to the post-re-peg base.

The 2028 re-peg therefore cannot circumvent v13 by design, and the operative text leaves no ambiguity. The re-peg increases the *input* to the multiplication, which benefits councillors. It does not reduce the decrement or exempt the new base from its application.

**The only circumvention scenario:** The 2028 re-peg could be structured to also expressly amend s. 4.3 to reset the accumulated decrement to zero. But that would be an express repeal/amendment of s. 4.3 — visible, recorded, politically accountable, and subject to the same public process as any other Policy amendment. Section 4.3(g) in-fine states: "any such amendment or repeal must be done through a properly-passed by-law and is subject to the same public process as any amendment to this Policy." This language ensures that any attempt to quietly piggyback a decrement-reset onto the 2028 re-peg would require a separate, named legislative act.

## Section 13 — Deputy Mayor / Budget Chair Stipends

**Finding: CONFIRMED — forward-looking definition cleanly covers future stipends**

Section 4.3(a)(v) defines "remuneration" to include "the base compensation of an elected official as set under this Policy, and any additional stipend or allowance paid to an elected official under this Policy as it may be amended from time to time, including without limitation any stipend paid to the Deputy Mayor or Budget Chair."

The London Council Guide confirms that the 8% Deputy Mayor/Budget Chair stipend mechanism is to be formalized in the Policy. Once so formalized (as Resolution 2025-C06 Part (h) directs), the stipend will automatically fall within the s. 4.3(a)(v) definition and the accumulated decrement will apply multiplicatively on top of it.[24]

The phrase "as it may be amended from time to time" is a standard forward-looking drafting device. It is effective to extend the decrement to stipends that do not yet exist in the Policy, provided they are eventually incorporated into the Policy through the normal amendment process. There is no gap or ambiguity here.

**One drafting note:** The "including without limitation" language is appropriate. It makes clear that the Deputy Mayor and Budget Chair examples are illustrative, not exhaustive. Any future stipend added to the Policy (e.g., a committee chair stipend) would automatically be covered without a further amendment to s. 4.3.

## Section 14 — Mayor Coverage

**Finding: CONFIRMED**

Section 4.3(a)(v) defines "remuneration" as covering "each City of London elected official." The Mayor is an elected official of the City of London within the standard meaning of that phrase under the *Municipal Act*. The Council Guide confirms the Mayor's compensation is governed by the same Policy CPOL.-70(b)-220. There is no carve-out or exception for the Mayor in any provision of v13. The Mayor is covered on the same terms as all 15 members of Council.[24][1]

## Section 15 — International / Canadian Precedents

**Finding: UNVERIFIABLE — no directly analogous precedents found**

Exhaustive research across Ontario, other Canadian provinces, and common-law international jurisdictions (United Kingdom, New Zealand, Australia) did not locate any municipal compensation by-law that uses a persistent-decrement mechanism tied to a fiscal indicator (levy, tax rate, deficit, or similar).

The closest analogs in Ontario are:
- **Index-linked compensation formulas**: Very common (e.g., CPI-linked, Statistics Canada median income-linked as in London's own s. 4.2). These establish automatic annual adjustments but do not create persistent ledger states.
- **Multi-year budget-linked compensation**: Rare but present in some Ontario municipalities (e.g., linking compensation reviews to budget cycles). These are review triggers, not persistent computational states.

The South Stormont by-law reviewed in research sets remuneration as a dollar figure adjusted annually by CPI — structurally similar to s. 4.2 but without any levy-linked modifier. No persistent ledger mechanism appears.[25]

**Political implication:** The absence of precedent is a double-edged sword. It prevents opponents from pointing to a failed example. But it also means proponents cannot cite a successful one. The novelty of the mechanism should be acknowledged proactively in any public communication strategy.

## Section 16 — Drafting Defects

**Finding: IMPRECISE — two defects require fixing; three additional improvements recommended**

### Defect 1 (ISSUE — Fix Required): Fiscal Year / Calendar Year Alignment

Section 4.3 consistently uses "fiscal year" without defining it. The City of London's fiscal year runs from January 1 to December 31 (consistent with s. 285 of the *Municipal Act*, which provides that the fiscal year of every municipality is a calendar year). However, s. 4.3 does not cross-reference s. 285 or the City's budget cycle, and the term "fiscal year" in the definitions section of the Policy (if one exists) may not resolve this.[1]

More importantly, s. 4.3(f) states the section "first applies to the remuneration payable for the first fiscal year that begins on or after the date this by-law comes into force." If v13 is introduced and passed in, say, September 2026, it would first apply to the 2027 fiscal year (January 1–December 31, 2027). The first year's accumulated decrement would be zero (per s. 4.3(f)), and the first levy change percentage applied would be the 2027 levy vs. the 2026 levy. This sequencing should be made explicit.

**Suggested fix:** Add to s. 4.3(a): "vi. 'Fiscal year' for the purposes of this section means the calendar year January 1 to December 31, as the City of London's fiscal year within the meaning of section 285 of the *Municipal Act, 2001*."

### Defect 2 (ISSUE — Fix Required): Timing of Annual Decrement Update

Section 4.3(b) states the decrement "shall be updated" for "each fiscal year." Section 4.3(e) requires annual calculation. But the by-law does not specify *when* in the fiscal year the decrement update must be performed — before or after the January 1 pay reset under s. 4.2.

The calculation depends on the annual rating by-law for the current year, which is typically passed in late winter or early spring (after the budget is adopted). This means the decrement for Year N cannot be calculated until the Year N annual rating by-law is passed — potentially months into Year N. Meanwhile, s. 4.2 adjusts pay on January 1 of Year N.

The practical consequence: payroll for January–March of each year would need to use a provisional (prior-year) decrement and then be reconciled once the new rating by-law is adopted.

**Suggested fix:** Add a clause to s. 4.3(e): "Where the annual rating by-law for a fiscal year has not been adopted before the first remuneration payment date in that fiscal year, the City Treasurer shall use the previous fiscal year's accumulated decrement for interim remuneration calculations, and shall reconcile any difference in the first remuneration payment following adoption of the annual rating by-law."

### Improvement 1 (IMPRECISE — Optional): Section 4.2 Independence — Compounding Clarification

As noted in Section 7, add to s. 4.3(c)(i): "and which does not include any reduction attributable to the accumulated decrement from prior or current fiscal years."

### Improvement 2 (IMPRECISE — Optional): Treasurer Non-Compliance Reconciliation Clause

As noted in Section 8, add a reconciliation clause to s. 4.3(e) addressing late calculation.

### Improvement 3 (IMPRECISE — Design Option): Decrement Cap Consideration

As noted in Section 9, consider whether a maximum decrement cap (e.g., 50% or 75%) should be included for political optics and legal conservatism, while preserving the accountability mechanism's practical force. This is a policy design choice, not a legal requirement.

## Section 17 — Political / Persuasive Vulnerability

**Finding: Identified — three lines of attack, with counter-framings**

### Attack 1: "This is an unconstitutional fetter on Council's legislative discretion"

**Opponents will argue:** By making every levy vote affect councillor pay, v13 effectively punishes councillors for exercising their legislative judgment to raise taxes. A future council facing necessary expenditures (infrastructure emergency, public health crisis) will be deterred from making the fiscally correct decision because of the personal pay consequence. This is an impermissible bias in the legislative process.

**Counter-framing:** The by-law does not compel any specific levy decision. Council retains full authority to raise the levy at any level it deems necessary — v13 simply ensures that councillors share the fiscal burden of those decisions with the taxpayers who bear them. The accountability mechanism is identical in principle to the performance-based compensation that is standard in both the private and public sectors. Moreover, s. 4.3(g) is explicit that Council's levy authority is "not restricted or altered."

### Attack 2: "The unbounded decrement is a punitive trap designed to reduce pay to zero"

**Opponents will argue:** A mechanism with no upper cap on the decrement is not a balanced accountability tool — it is a punitive mechanism designed to drive pay down indefinitely. In a high-inflation environment where levy increases are driven by provincial mandates, downloading, and cost pressures beyond Council's control, councillors would be penalised for factors outside their control.

**Counter-framing:** The mechanism is symmetrical — levy decreases erode the decrement. It tracks the net change in the levy over time, not any single year's increase. The worked example demonstrates that a single large levy reduction (e.g., -8% in Year 6) can wipe out a multi-year accumulated decrement. Councillors control the levy directly; they control the mechanism's long-run trajectory. If provincial downloading forces levy increases, the same councillors retain the power to advocate for and achieve provincial funding adjustments. The mechanism creates a fiscal incentive to push back on downloading.

### Attack 3: "This intrudes on the authority of future democratically elected councils"

**Opponents will argue:** A mechanism passed by the current Council that automatically constrains the pay of future elected councils — who never voted for it — is an anti-democratic intrusion on future democratic mandates. Incoming councillors in 2026 and beyond will be bound by a pay formula they had no role in establishing.

**Counter-framing:** This argument proves too much — by the same logic, no municipality could ever establish a multi-year budget, a reserve fund policy, or a compensation indexing formula, since all of these prospectively structure the choices available to future councils. The *Municipal Act* explicitly contemplates ongoing by-laws (s. 5(2): "Anything begun by one council may be continued and completed by a succeeding council"). Future councils retain the right to repeal or amend s. 4.3 through the same democratic process. The mechanism is not entrenched; it is simply in force until repealed — like any other by-law.[1]

## Section 18 — Additional Exposure, Defects, and Improvement Opportunities

### 18.1 CPOL.-70(b)-220 Integration Point

The operative text amends the Policy by inserting s. 4.3, but the by-law does not specify whether s. 4.3 replaces v12's entire text or is inserted alongside any surviving v12 provisions. The query text states "v13 replaces v12 entirely" — confirm this is reflected in the operative enacting clause of the full by-law (not just the s. 4.3 text provided). An enacting clause like "Section 4.3 of Policy CPOL.-70(b)-220 is hereby repealed and the following substituted therefor" is necessary.

### 18.2 Coming-Into-Force Date Coordination

The resolution 2025-C06 Part (a) resets the base effective November 15, 2026 (start of next Council term). If v13 comes into force before the start of the 2027 fiscal year (e.g., it is passed in Fall 2026), the first application under s. 4.3(f) will be to the 2027 fiscal year (first January 1 following the coming-into-force date). This means the 2026 fiscal year — including the November 15 start of the new pay level — is not subject to the accumulated decrement. This is likely the correct design intent (the decrement starts clean at the beginning of the new Council term's first full fiscal year), but it should be confirmed and, if intended, made explicit in a recital.[24]

### 18.3 OMERS Pension Interaction

Councillors participate in OMERS, Ontario's municipal defined-benefit pension plan. OMERS contributions and ultimately pension benefits are calculated based on "contributory earnings," which for elected officials is generally their base remuneration. If the accumulated decrement reduces the remuneration figure on which OMERS contributions are calculated, this will reduce both the councillor's contribution and the City's matching contribution — and potentially reduce the pension entitlement accruing in high-decrement years. This is likely an intended (and legally permissible) consequence, but the City's OMERS administrator should be notified in advance so payroll systems are configured correctly.

### 18.4 Multi-Year Budget Cycle Interaction

London adopted a 2024–2027 Multi-Year Budget. The annual rating by-law for each year within the cycle implements the budgeted levy. For PACT purposes, the "total municipal tax levy" is defined as "the amount to be levied … as originally adopted by Council in the annual rating by-law for that year." The 2024–2027 Multi-Year Budget anticipated levy increases of ~7.5% average per year. Under v13, these increases would generate significant accumulated decrement in early years of a new Council term. This is consistent with the mechanism's design but should be factored into the PACT campaign's public communication (the 2024-2027 budget's 7.5% average levy increases are on the record and can be used as a concrete worked example).[26]

London's 2026 levy increase was adopted at approximately 3.4–3.9% (with some reserve application). This is the first real-world data point available for the PACT mechanism's worked examples.[27][28]
