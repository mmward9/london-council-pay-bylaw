## Part 1: BLOCKER 1 — "Notwithstanding" Override

### Interpretive Principles for Intra-Instrument "Notwithstanding" Clauses

The use of "notwithstanding" within a single municipal by-law instrument to cause a later provision to supersede an earlier one is recognized drafting practice in Ontario. The governing interpretive doctrine in Ontario municipal law is that by-law interpretation follows the same statutory interpretation principles as legislation generally. The leading statement is from the Ontario courts: "interpretation of municipal bylaws adopts the same doctrine as statutory interpretation generally."[^1]

The specific principle applicable here is *generalia specialibus non derogant* in reverse: where a later, more specific provision in the same instrument expressly states it overrides an earlier provision, the later provision controls. The word "notwithstanding" functions as an express override directive. Courts applying this principle to intra-instrument conflicts consistently hold that the "notwithstanding" clause removes any ambiguity about which provision governs. The Supreme Court of Canada's *Auer v. Auer* decision (2024), affirmed in Ontario Court of Appeal application, reinforced that subordinate legislation (including municipal by-laws) is reviewed on a reasonableness standard and benefits from a presumption of validity — challengers must show the by-law "does not fall within a reasonable interpretation of the delegate's statutory authority." This materially favours PACT: a court reviewing section 4.3(b)'s "notwithstanding" override would start from validity presumed, not invalidity presumed.[^2]

**Key point for counsel brief:** The "notwithstanding subsections 4.2(a) and 4.2(b)" language in section 4.3(b) is textbook municipal drafting. It creates an unambiguous hierarchy: 4.3(b) governs over both 4.2(a) (the zero floor) and 4.2(b) (the wage-freeze pause) in the specific factual scenario where the levy increase percentage is positive. There is no reported Ontario case striking down an intra-instrument "notwithstanding" clause of this type. The risk counsel must address is not the drafting technique itself but rather the *substance* of what is being authorised — specifically whether the Municipal Act permits remuneration to be reduced by formula. That is BLOCKER 3.

### Does 4.2(b)'s "No Increase Shall Be Applied" Suppress Positive Adjustments Only?

The operative language in section 4.2(b) of CPOL.-70(b)-220 is: "in those years where non-union staff wages are frozen, no increase shall be applied."

Plain reading under Ontario statutory interpretation principles: "no increase shall be applied" suppresses only positive adjustments. "Increase" is not synonymous with "adjustment." A negative adjustment is a decrease, not an increase. Had the drafters of 4.2(b) intended to pause all adjustments — up or down — they would have said "no adjustment shall be applied" or "the remuneration shall remain unchanged." The choice of the narrower word "increase" in a provision titled or functioning as a wage-freeze pause provision indicates suppression of upward movement only.

This reading is supported by the general principle that penal or restrictive provisions (provisions that limit a power otherwise conferred) are read narrowly in Ontario. "No increase shall be applied" does not, on its face, authorize or require a downward adjustment — it merely removes the obligation to apply an upward one. PACT's section 4.3(b), operating under its "notwithstanding" override, independently authorizes the downward adjustment in years when the levy increase percentage is positive. The two provisions operate on distinct subject matter.

**Practical consequence for the counsel brief:** This interpretation means that section 4.2(b) presents a *zero barrier* to the downward adjustment mechanism, not even a potential conflict to override. The "notwithstanding 4.2(b)" language in section 4.3(b) is therefore belt-and-suspenders — it is legally correct but arguably unnecessary. Counsel should confirm this reading, but it is low-risk. The higher-risk question remains whether the Municipal Act authorises the downward adjustment at all (BLOCKER 3).

## Part 2: BLOCKER 2 — "Total Municipal Tax Levy" Mapping to London's Annual Rating By-Law

### London's Annual Tax Levy: Scale and Budget Context

The City of London's 2024–2027 Multi-Year Budget projects the following tax levy revenue (municipal purposes only, i.e., property tax supported budget):[^3]

| Fiscal Year | Tax Levy Revenue ($ thousands) | Year-over-Year Increase (from rates) |
|-------------|-------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| 2023 Budget | $736,458 | — |
| 2024 Budget | $812,743 | 8.7% |
| 2025 Budget | $883,399 | 8.7% |
| 2026 Budget | $933,567 | 5.7% |
| 2027 Budget | $995,961 | 6.7% |

These are the approved multi-year figures as of March 2024. The 2025 Annual Budget Update (adopted November 2024 at 7.3% tax increase) and the 2026 Annual Budget Update (adopted December 2025 at approximately 3.4%) represent Council-adopted amendments to the base figures. The annual rating by-law passed under s. 312(2) of the Municipal Act locks in the final levy figure for each year.[^4][^5][^6][^3]

### How Ontario Annual Rating By-Laws Are Structured (Structural Template)

A review of comparable Ontario municipal annual rating by-laws — including the Middlesex Centre 2025 Tax Rate By-law (By-law 2025-100) passed immediately adjacent to London, and the general structure prescribed by the Ontario Financial Information Return Schedule 22 — reveals the standard architecture of an Ontario annual rating by-law passed under s. 312(2):[^7]

1. **Recitals:** Council authority under Municipal Act s. 312(2); the budget by-law adopted; and the Ontario Education Act / O. Reg. 400/98 as the authority for the education levy rates (set by the province).[^8]
2. **Schedule A — Tax Rates:** A table listing property classes (Residential, Multi-Residential, Commercial, Industrial, Farmland, etc.) with three columns: the *municipal tax rate*, the *education tax rate* (provincially mandated), and the *combined rate* per $1,000 of assessment.
3. **Whereas or Recital identifying the total levy required:** Many Ontario rating by-laws include in their body text or Schedule A the total dollar amount of the general local municipality levy to be raised ("the sum of $X is required for municipal purposes").
4. **Separate identification of education taxes:** Ontario rating by-laws are required to levy education taxes at the rates set by the province separately from municipal rates. O. Reg. 400/98 sets school board rates. The provincial education levy appears as a distinct column and is separately identifiable from the municipal purposes column in the rate schedule.[^8][^7]
5. **No standard "MPAC assessment growth" line:** MPAC-driven assessment growth does not appear as a separately identified dollar figure in the annual rating by-law itself. It appears implicitly: the tax rate is applied to the total assessment roll, so if assessment grows, revenue grows at the same rate. The increase attributable specifically to MPAC-driven assessment growth is therefore a *derived figure* (new assessment × rate), not a standalone line item in the annual rating by-law text.

### BLOCKER 2 Analysis and Drafting Risk

The critical risk for PACT's section 4.3(a)(ii) definition of "total municipal tax levy" is the **MPAC exclusion clause**: "any amount identified as arising from changes in assessment values determined by the Municipal Property Assessment Corporation."

Based on the standard Ontario rating by-law architecture, that amount is **not separately identified** as a line item in London's annual rating by-law. The total levy figure in the rating by-law will be the gross levy required for municipal purposes at the adopted tax rates applied to the then-current assessment roll. It will not break out "base growth from rates" versus "revenue from new MPAC assessment." That distinction exists in the budget documents (the budget reports quantify assessment growth revenue separately from rate increases) but not in the by-law itself.[^3]

**The consequence:** Under PACT's current drafting, the fallback clause applies — "If any such amount is not separately identified in the annual rating by-law, the total municipal tax levy is the figure as presented in the by-law for municipal purposes without further reduction." This means the MPAC exclusion will almost certainly never activate, and the "total municipal tax levy" will simply equal the gross municipal purposes total in the by-law. This is workable but creates a minor integrity argument: PACT purports to measure Council's *fiscal decisions* (tax rate increases), but if assessment growth inflates the gross levy figure without any rate decision by Council, the PACT denominator includes a component Council did not actively choose. In practice, this makes PACT slightly more favourable to councillors (the levy increase percentage is inflated by assessment growth, making the reduction larger than the "policy-only" rate increase), which cuts against the "punitive" framing.

**Recommended action for counsel / Treasurer:** The Treasurer should confirm (a) whether London's annual rating by-law contains a total-levy-required recital specifying the gross municipal purposes levy as a single dollar figure; (b) whether that figure includes or excludes supplementary assessments at time of passage; and (c) whether the education tax component is a separately identifiable column in Schedule A of the rating by-law, enabling its exclusion from the "municipal purposes" total. If the answer to (a) and (c) is yes — which is the standard practice — PACT's definition works cleanly for the education exclusion and tolerates the MPAC issue through the fallback clause.

**Schedule naming:** London's annual rating by-law, once obtained, should be reviewed for its schedule naming convention. Ontario municipalities commonly title the rates schedule "Schedule A" or "Schedule 1 — Tax Rates." PACT's section 4.3(a)(ii) does not need to specify the schedule name if it relies on the "as presented in the by-law for municipal purposes" fallback, but tightening the reference to, for example, "the total amount levied for municipal purposes as identified in Schedule A (or equivalent) of the annual rating by-law" would improve precision. This is a Level-2 drafting refinement, not a BLOCKER.

## Part 3: BLOCKER 3 — Municipal Act Authority for Formula-Driven Downward Remuneration

### Statutory Authority Base

PACT's amending by-law correctly identifies its authority as ss. 5(3), 8(1), and 11(2) paragraphs 1 and 3 of the Municipal Act, 2001. Section 8(1)'s broad-interpretation mandate is the key provision. Section 8(2) adds: "In the event of ambiguity in whether or not a municipality has the authority... the ambiguity shall be resolved so as to include, rather than exclude" the power.[^9]

### Standard of Judicial Review After *Auer v. Auer* (2024)

The November 2024 Supreme Court decisions in *Auer v. Auer* and *TransAlta Generation Partnership v. Alberta* settled the standard of review for subordinate legislation including municipal by-laws at reasonableness. Under the old *Katz* standard, a challenger had to show the by-law was "irrelevant, extraneous or completely unrelated" to the enabling statute's objectives — an extremely high threshold. Under *Auer*, the test is whether the by-law "does not fall within a reasonable interpretation of the delegate's statutory authority." This is a lower threshold for challengers than *Katz*, but the presumption of validity remains, and courts are directed to construe subordinate legislation in a manner that renders it *intra vires* where possible.[^2]

For PACT, the *Auer* test applied to s. 8(1) authority means: is it reasonable to interpret s. 8(1) as conferring authority to enact a formula-driven downward remuneration adjustment tied to levy decisions? The answer is highly likely yes. The by-law's WHEREAS clauses anchor the mechanism in fiscal accountability, alignment between remuneration and the municipality's fiscal capacity decisions, and public confidence — all of which map onto s. 11(2)'s "financial management" and "governance structure" heads of authority. A court applying *Auer* would be unlikely to find this an unreasonable interpretation of the delegate's statutory authority.[^9]

### Ontario Precedent on Downward Remuneration

No specific Ontario case was located in which a council remuneration by-law was successfully challenged as *ultra vires* for producing a downward adjustment. The absence of such precedent cuts both ways: there is no supporting authority but also no adverse authority. The absence likely reflects that formula-driven downward adjustments are uncommon in Ontario remuneration practice rather than that they have been challenged and failed.

The typical Ontario municipal remuneration by-law either (a) sets amounts at a fixed level, (b) provides for upward-only CPI indexing, or (c) provides for periodic resets by resolution. Formula-driven decrements tied to fiscal indicators appear to be genuinely novel in Ontario municipal practice. This novelty is itself the reason BLOCKER 3 requires counsel confirmation: not because there is adverse authority, but because there is no precedent directly on point.[^10][^11]

**Counsel brief framing:** The absence of an explicit specific grant of authority for downward adjustments in the Municipal Act is fully covered by s. 8(1) and s. 8(2). The broad interpretation mandate in s. 8 was specifically enacted to eliminate the need to find a specific enabling provision for each municipal action. The mechanism is also not punitive in structure — it is a formula adjustment that applies symmetrically (it cannot produce a larger reduction than the levy increase, and it only applies in levy-increase years). The WHEREAS clause anchoring in fiscal accountability provides the rational connection to municipal governance purposes required to survive reasonableness review.

## Part 4: BLOCKER 3 (Subsidiary) — Municipal Conflict of Interest Act Analysis

### Section 4(i) — The Self-Remuneration Exemption

The *Municipal Conflict of Interest Act*, R.S.O. 1990, c. M.50, requires a member who has a pecuniary interest in a matter to disclose that interest and not vote. A vote on PACT — which would affect each councillor's own future remuneration — constitutes a direct pecuniary interest. However, s. 4(i) of the MCIA provides a complete exemption:[^12]

> Sections 5, 5.2 and 5.3 **do not apply** to a pecuniary interest... **(i)** in respect of an allowance for attendance at meetings, or any other allowance, honorarium, **remuneration**, salary or benefit to which the member may be entitled **by reason of being a member**...[^12]

This provision directly and unambiguously applies to PACT. Each councillor's pecuniary interest in PACT arises "by reason of being a member." The exemption in s. 4(i) therefore removes any conflict of interest disqualification for the vote on introduction, debate, or passage of the amending by-law. All fifteen members of Council (14 councillors plus the Mayor) may vote without disclosure or disqualification.[^12]

**One nuance for the counsel brief:** The Mayor's position is slightly different post-Bill 3. Section 5.3 of the MCIA governs the head of council's use of strong-mayor powers with respect to matters in which the Mayor has a pecuniary interest. Section 4(i) expressly exempts ss. 5, 5.2, *and 5.3* from applying to the remuneration interest. This means the Mayor may also use strong-mayor powers (including budget proposal powers) with respect to matters touching councillor remuneration, without MCIA exposure.[^12]

## Part 5: Strong-Mayor Veto Exposure — Confirmed Analysis

### O. Reg. 530/22 — Full Carve-Out Map

A full reading of O. Reg. 530/22 (retrieved directly from ontario.ca) confirms the following complete carve-out structure:[^13]

| Section | Subject | Veto Applicability to PACT |
|---------|---------|---------------------------|
| s. 5(1) | By-laws under ss. 289 and 290 of the Municipal Act (budget estimates by-laws) | Exempt from veto — **PACT is NOT a s. 289/290 by-law**, so this carve-out does not protect PACT |
| s. 5(2) | By-laws under s. 2 of the Development Charges Act, 1997 | Expressly veto-applicable — irrelevant to PACT |
| s. 7 | Annual budget process (proposed budget, council resolution, veto, override) | Governs budget cycle veto — PACT is not a budget by-law |
| s. 9 | In-year budget amendments under s. 311/312 of the Act | Governs in-year levy amendments — PACT is not a levy by-law |
| s. 10 | Commencement | No operational veto content |

**Conclusion:** There is no carve-out in O. Reg. 530/22 that exempts a remuneration amending by-law from the Mayor's veto power under s. 284.11 of the Municipal Act. PACT is subject to veto.[^13]

### O. Reg. 580/22 — Prescribed Provincial Priorities (Full Text)

O. Reg. 580/22, retrieved directly from ontario.ca, prescribes exactly two provincial priorities for purposes of ss. 284.10, 284.11, and 284.11.1 of the Act:[^14]

> 1. Building 1.5 million new residential units by December 31, 2031.
> 2. Constructing and maintaining infrastructure to support housing, including (i) transit, (ii) roads, (iii) utilities, and (iv) servicing.[^14]

**PACT veto risk from prescribed priorities: extremely low.** A remuneration by-law that adjusts the annual percentage increase to councillor pay cannot plausibly "interfere with" either the housing-units priority or the housing-infrastructure priority. The only arguable connection would be an extremely strained argument that PACT's fiscal restraint orientation might affect Council's appetite for housing-related capital spending — but this is three steps removed from the by-law's text and would not survive reasonableness review. Mayor Morgan would need to provide written reasons for the veto (s. 284.11(5)), and a veto of PACT on housing grounds would be legally vulnerable and politically extraordinary.[^15][^14]

### Two-Thirds Override

If the Mayor were to veto PACT, the override threshold under s. 284.11(9) is two-thirds of council (including the Mayor, per s. 284.11(10)). London Council has 15 members. Two-thirds is 10 votes. The November 4, 2025 vote on resolution 2025-C06 produced 9 in favour and 6 against. To override a mayoral veto, PACT would need the original 9 supporters plus one conversion from the 6 who voted no — or the Mayor's own vote plus 9 others. Given Mayor Morgan was among the 9 who voted *for* the pay raise, a mayoral veto of PACT would be followed by the Mayor voting to override his own veto (as he would be permitted to do under s. 284.11(10)), making the override path mathematically plausible if the coalition holds.[^15]

## Part 7: Additional Findings Not Raised in Prior Audit Rounds

### Finding A — O. Reg. 530/22's Section 1 Designates Ottawa, Not London, for s. 284.2

A precise reading of O. Reg. 530/22 s. 1 reveals it designates **the City of Ottawa** for the purpose of s. 284.2 of the Act (the head of council's power to appoint and dismiss the CAO). London is designated in Schedule 1 for the general application of Part VI.1 — but the **s. 284.2 appointment/dismissal power applies only to Ottawa**. This is already correct in v12 (v12 identifies London as #106 in Schedule 1 for the general veto framework), but it is worth noting explicitly: London's Mayor does not have the s. 284.2 CAO appointment/dismissal power that Ottawa's Mayor has. This distinction has no bearing on PACT but is relevant background for the campaign's general framing of strong-mayor powers.[^13]

### Finding B — "Levy Increase Percentage" Includes Assessment Growth: A Policy Framing Opportunity

As noted in the BLOCKER 2 analysis, the "total municipal tax levy" under PACT will include revenue from assessment growth (new construction, etc.), not just from rate decisions. In years of high assessment growth (which London has experienced during the 2024–2027 budget cycle), the levy can increase even if Council holds the *rate* flat. Under PACT, a rate-flat year with 2% assessment growth would produce a levy increase percentage of approximately 2%, triggering a 2-percentage-point reduction to the section 4.2 adjustment. This is arguably a *feature* from a fiscal accountability standpoint — Council's remuneration growth is moderated whenever the tax burden on residents increases, regardless of cause — but it could also be framed as a *flaw* by opponents who argue the mechanism penalises councillors for provincial assessment decisions rather than for Council's own fiscal choices. The campaign should be prepared to address this framing. The clean response is that the *total municipal tax levy* is the direct measurement of what property owners actually pay to the City for municipal purposes, and PACT ties councillor remuneration to that burden regardless of source. If Council wanted to offset assessment growth effects, it could pass a rate reduction — that is itself a Council decision.

### Finding C — Section 4.3(f) Priority/Conflict Clause Should Reference "Section 4" Only, Not "Section 4 of This Policy"

Minor drafting note: subsection 4.3(f) states the priority clause "applies only to interpretation conflicts within section 4 of this Policy relating to annual adjustment and levy-linkage." This is appropriately narrow. However, if CPOL.-70(b)-220 is structured so that "section 4" covers all annual adjustment provisions and is numbered differently in the original instrument, there is a risk of mis-citation. The recommended action is for the City Solicitor to confirm the exact section numbering of the adjustment provisions in the current CPOL.-70(b)-220 text and ensure 4.3(f)'s cross-reference matches precisely.

### Finding D — "First January 1 Occurring After This By-law Comes into Force" (Section 4.3(d)) — Election Cycle Timing

Section 4.3(d) provides that PACT "first applies to the annual adjustment under section 4.2 calculated for the first January 1 occurring after this by-law comes into force." If PACT is introduced and passed in the October–November 2026 term (the new Council's first full term), and it receives Royal Assent/passage in, say, November 2026, it would first apply to the January 1, 2027 adjustment. The levy figure used for that adjustment would be the 2026 levy (from the 2026 annual rating by-law) compared to the 2025 levy. Given the 2026 tax increase was approximately 3.4% and the 2025 tax increase was approximately 7.3%, the first PACT adjustment would be calculated against the 3.4% 2026 levy increase — reducing the January 1, 2027 remuneration adjustment by approximately 3.4 percentage points. This creates a concrete, immediate, and publicly communicable effect. The campaign should have this worked example ready.[^5][^6][^4]

### Finding E — London's 2025 Tax Levy Figure for PACT Calculation

From the approved 2024–2027 Multi-Year Budget: the 2025 budgeted municipal tax levy revenue is **$883,399 thousand** ($883.4 million), up from $812,743 thousand in 2024, representing an 8.7% increase from rates. The 2025 Annual Budget Update (November 2024) approved a 7.3% tax increase for 2025 — this figure (7.3%) would be closer to the actual levy increase percentage under PACT for the year in which this calculation would first be relevant. The Treasurer confirmation (BLOCKER 2) will establish the exact dollar figures from the annual rating by-laws.[^4][^3]
