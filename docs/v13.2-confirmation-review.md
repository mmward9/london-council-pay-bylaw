### Q1 — Lateness Trigger Anchor (Change A)

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

The trigger *"has not been completed before the date on which the relevant annual rating by-law is adopted"* is a defined, observable, recorded event — the adoption date of the annual rating by-law is inscribed in the by-law itself and in the minutes of the Council meeting at which it passes. One note for completeness: "adopted" should be read consistently with standard Ontario municipal practice as the date of passage of the annual rating by-law at Council (not the date of publication, certification, or coming into force, if those differ). In London's practice these are typically the same date, but if there is ever a gap (e.g., a by-law passed in open session but signed by the Clerk the following day), "adopted" should be interpreted as the date of the Council vote. This is resolved by the standard rules of interpretation for Ontario by-laws and requires no amendment — flagged only as an administrative awareness point.

### Q2 — Sequencing Priority: "Without Limiting Subsection (iv)" (Change B)

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

The prepended *"without limiting subsection (iv)"* is the standard Ontario drafting formula for establishing lex specialis / lex generalis hierarchy between two adjacent provisions. It preserves (iv)'s primacy over the overlap scenario (rating-by-law not adopted before first payment date) while activating (v) for all other lateness scenarios — including the scenario where the rating by-law is adopted on time but the Treasurer's calculation or publication duty is performed late. ### Q3 — 60-Day Hard Backstop (Change C) — Adoption and Enforceability

**Verdict: CONFIRMED — the 60-day window is adopted and enforceable as drafted, with one operational observation**

The phrase *"as soon as practicable and in any event no later than 60 days after the end of the relevant fiscal year"* is a well-established two-tier deadline structure in Ontario public law: best efforts first, hard backstop second. Courts enforce the hard backstop as an absolute obligation where "as soon as practicable" has not been met. The 60-day figure (by March 1 for a December 31 fiscal year-end) is operationally realistic — the City's annual budget process typically concludes in late February, and the annual rating by-law for the prior year's final levy is on the record before then.

**One operational observation:** The 60-day backstop in (v) interacts with the City Treasurer's existing obligation under *Municipal Act* s. 294.1 to provide Council with a statement of remuneration and expenses paid to members by March 31 of the following year. The v13.2 60-day backstop (by March 1) is slightly earlier than the s. 294.1 March 31 deadline, which is appropriate — the accumulated decrement calculation should precede and inform the s. 294.1 statement, not follow it. No conflict; the sequencing is correct.

### Q5 — New Exposure Introduced by v13.2 Changes

#### Q5(a) — Four-Element Compound Sentence in New (v)

**Verdict: CONFIRMED — no internal ambiguity**

The four elements parse cleanly:

1. **Lateness trigger:** *"has not been completed before the date on which the relevant annual rating by-law is adopted"* — a single defined event, not a range.
2. **Catch-up duty with two-deadline standard:** *"perform … as soon as practicable and in any event no later than 60 days after the end of the relevant fiscal year"* — the two-tier standard is internally consistent; the hard backstop does not conflict with the best-efforts standard because the hard backstop is logically subsequent to and a ceiling on the best-efforts duty.
3. **Retroactive application:** *"apply the resulting accumulated decrement retroactively to the remuneration for the relevant fiscal year"* — "resulting" correctly ties the retroactive application to the accumulated decrement calculated under the catch-up, not to any interim figure.
4. **Reconciliation:** *"with any over- or under-payment reconciled in the next remuneration payment period following the calculation"* — the trigger for reconciliation is "following the calculation," which is the correct reference point (not following adoption of the rating by-law, which is already covered by (iv)).

One cross-check: where the rating by-law is adopted late (after the first payment date), both (iv) and (v) are technically triggered. (iv) applies as lex specialis and governs the interim-calculation and adoption-triggered reconciliation. (v) is excluded from that scenario by the "without limiting subsection (iv)" prepend. If the Treasurer then also fails to complete the formal calculation record within 60 days of fiscal year-end, (v) re-activates as to the recording and publication duties under (ii) and (iii), while (iv) continues to govern the payment-amount reconciliation. This tiered activation is logical and non-contradictory — the two provisions cover distinct duties in the overlap scenario without prescribing conflicting outcomes.

#### Q5(b) — Interaction Between 60-Day Backstop and OMERS Reconciliation

**Verdict: IMPRECISE — not a drafting defect in v13.2, but a coordination item requiring administrative action**

The OMERS issue flagged in v13.1 Q7.2 is not worsened by v13.2. The 60-day backstop (by March 1) actually improves the OMERS situation relative to an open-ended "as soon as practicable" standard: OMERS employer contribution remittances for a calendar year are typically reconciled in the first quarter of the following year, and a March 1 final-calculation date gives the payroll function approximately 30–60 days to process a reconciliation adjustment before the OMERS annual remittance deadline (typically March 31). That determination is made under the OMERS *Act* and plan rules, not by v13.2. No amendment to v13.2 is warranted; the OMERS administrator briefing recommended in v13 Section 18.3 remains the correct action.

### Q7 — Any Other Defects, Improvement Opportunities, or Concerns

Only one observation surfaces — and it is narrow:

**7.1 — The 60-Day Backstop Is Asymmetric With Respect to the Fiscal Year Direction**

The backstop reads *"no later than 60 days after the end of the relevant fiscal year."* For a December 31 fiscal year-end this yields a March 1 deadline. This works in normal operation. However, *"the end of the relevant fiscal year"* as a reference point only makes logical sense if the lateness extends beyond the fiscal year-end. If the annual rating by-law is adopted late — say, in mid-February of the current fiscal year — but the Treasurer completes the calculation in late February (before the fiscal year-end of December 31), the backstop clause is never triggered and the provision works correctly through the "as soon as practicable" limb alone.

The edge case is: the annual rating by-law is adopted very late in the fiscal year (say, November) and the Treasurer does not complete the calculation before December 31. In that scenario, the backstop gives the Treasurer until March 1 of the *following* year — which is after the fiscal year to which the decrement applies has ended. The retroactive application and reconciliation mechanism handles this correctly. No drafting fix is needed; the mechanism is logically complete. This is purely an awareness note.
