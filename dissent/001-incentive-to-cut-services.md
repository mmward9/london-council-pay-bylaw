## The argument

The bylaw rewards council with a {{ policy.asymmetry.levy_cut_pay_adjustment | replace('_', ' ') }} adjustment when the operating tax levy decreases. A self-interested council could meet that condition by deferring road maintenance, cutting library hours, freezing emergency-services hiring, and starving social programs. Voters get a temporary tax cut and a longer-term service decay; council pockets a raise. The bylaw turns council against its own constituents.

## The response

The bylaw caps the pay-rise side of the formula at CPI plus one percentage point. The maximum a council can give itself in a single year, no matter how aggressively it cuts, is one percentage point above inflation. That is small relative to the reputational and electoral cost of visibly degraded services.

The pay-cut side, by contrast, is uncapped except by the floor. A council that tries to game the formula by cutting services aggressively in one year will face voter backlash in the same election cycle. The asymmetry of the formula deliberately makes "cut services to chase a raise" a bad deal: you get one percentage point above CPI for one year and pay for it at the next election.

The bylaw also does not require council to cut services. A council that finds genuine operational efficiencies, restructures contracts, or pushes back against provincial cost downloads can hold the levy flat and still receive an inflation match. The path of least resistance under this bylaw is competent management, not service decay.

If a future council is willing to wreck services for a one-percentage-point bonus, that council has problems that no compensation structure can fix. The voters are the ultimate constraint.
